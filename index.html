About Me

I work as an after-school educator at Deep Cove out of school care and love engaging with young children. I volunteered at a daycare with kids aged 2–6, which taught me patience and emotional intelligence. I thrive in creative, people-focused environments and value balance and connection. Relational Walking Reflection

I bundled up and headed for a winter walk through Deep Cove. Looking back on this walk I am noticing how privileged I am to walk here because I know that deep cove is located in the Tsleil-Waututh Nations land. I noticed some birds chirping in the background in the morning. I touched a leafy branch that smelled like earthy leaves. I observed the frosty ground condition, and as I kept on walking, I saw some construction. The sound of the construction was interruptive - the land was dead, all the trees were removed, and an orange steel fence surrounded the lot. A huge orange excavator had torn up the soil. To escape this eyesore, I started walking down this hill, and as I got close to the beach, I heard the waves crashing. It reminded me of a steady drum rhythm, pulsing the water back and forth. Powerful waves made me feel so insignificant, in relation to the ocean. The waves were in and out. I smelled the fresh salty air. As I thumped along the beach in my winter boots, I saw a beautiful big piece of musty smelling wood, that must have been washed up by the tide to the shore. I walked around this gigantic piece of wood; it was massive, and the wood was intricately woven, and twisted together. Donald believes that walking in nature is a gift, and relational teachings he thinks are

“gifted practices, and their related wisdom teachings, are enmeshed within a kinship connectivity of original treaties between human beings and all other forms of life”

— Dwayne Donald, We Need a New Story: Walking and the wâhkôhtowin Imagination," Journal of the Canadian Association for Curriculum Studies (JCACS)Page 56, Vol. 18, No. 2, 2021

I couldn't agree with this more because through walking I always learn and observe new things from nature, and I gain respect and new perspectives from the people around me. Connections to the land is what makes Canada so unique, and in my opinion through walking, we can begin to connect with the nature itself and learn how to respect the people from the land.

Inequity of Public Space: Open Space for Older People

After learning more about the inequity of public space, particularly for marginalized people, I started thinking more openly about the Inclusivity and accessibility of public space. I recently went to Quesnel to visit my Opa, who is turning 91, and he is not able to walk without a cane or walker. I started thinking about how difficult it might be for older people to get outside and enjoy activities. He is pretty isolated, and this got me thinking about public open space for older people, particularly around social interactions and well-being.
